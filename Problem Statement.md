South Africa has a population of over 64 million but only 21 PET scanners available [1] – a far cry from the 2 per million people recommended by the World Health Organisation [2]. Of those, only 5 are in the state sector, which is responsible for treating 82% of patients. The primary reason for this undersupply of PET scanners is their prohibitively high cost of manufacturing. A major contributor to this cost is the large quantities of monolithic crystal scintillator material needed in the detector modules used to detect 511keV gamma rays. The development of nanocomposite scintillator materials has garnered substantial interest as a potential solution to this problem, however such materials are susceptible to high rates of self-absorption due to small nanoparticle [[Stoke's shift]] as well as agglomeration during synthesis. While fabrication methods exist to mitigate these factors, they drastically increase the complexity, and hence the cost, of manufacturing. As such, nanocomposite scintillators have yet to demonstrate feasible utility in real-world applications.

>[!tip] Thinking out loud - ways to reduce self-absorption
>- Core-shell NPs - NP shell band gap very different to core band gap, so doesn't preferentially become excited by self-emission. 
>- Wavelength shifters can increase Stoke's shift, but may also contribute to reabsorption. This is key! We ideally want to simplify the fabrication by using NPs that emit closer to peak SiPM efficiency, negating the need for wavelength shifters. PPO enhances light yield, but then POPOP is needed to shift wavelength away from excitation wavelength of PPO!
>- NP Homogeneity - Surface ligand exchange prevents agglomeration. Or *in-situ* NP synthesis
>- Polymer annealing to reduce opacity due to polymerization stress
>- Minimization of bubbles
>- Surface polishing
